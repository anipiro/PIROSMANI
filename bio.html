A Life in Shadows

From the village of Mirzaani to the basements of Tbilisi — the quiet, difficult life of a self-taught master.

Born around 1862 in the village of Mirzaani in Kakheti, Niko Pirosmanashvili lost his parents young and was raised by relatives in Tbilisi. He never received formal artistic training, and yet by his thirties he had developed a visual language so direct, so sincere, that it would come to define an entire nation's modern image of itself.

He earned his living by painting signboards for the dukhans — the small taverns of old Tiflis — and by composing portraits, feasts, and animals on cheap black oilcloth. His patrons paid him in food and shelter, rarely in coin.

Legend says he once fell in love with a French actress named Marguerite and sold everything he owned to flood the street outside her hotel with roses. Whether myth or memory, the story became immortal in Andrei Voznesensky's poem and Alla Pugacheva's song A Million Scarlet Roses.

He died in 1918, ill and impoverished, in a small Tbilisi basement. The exact location of his grave remains unknown.

A timeline

  1. c. 1862

    Born in Mirzaani

    In a small village in the wine-growing region of Kakheti, eastern Georgia.

  2. 1870s

    Orphaned, moves to Tbilisi

    Raised by the Kalantarov family; works as a servant and railway brakeman.

  3. 1890s

    Begins painting full-time

    Opens a small dairy shop; soon abandons commerce for the dukhans and his oilcloths.

  4. 1912

    Discovered by the avant-garde

    The Zdanevich brothers and Mikhail Le Dentu find his signboards and bring his name to Russian modernist circles.

  5. 1918

    Death in obscurity

    Dies in a Tbilisi basement, largely unrecognized in his lifetime.
